Global Water Pollution and Infant Mortality Analysis
Project Overview
This project investigates the relationship between environmental water quality and public health outcomes across 3,000 global regions. The analysis explores whether lead contamination in water sources is the primary driver of infant mortality, or if other variables, such as healthcare access, exert a more significant influence on survival rates.

Analytical Objectives
This capstone project focuses on bridging data science methodology with data-driven storytelling:

Data Pipeline (FINAL_PROJECT_2.ipynb): Utilized Python (Pandas) to clean and standardize a comprehensive dataset. This included repairing inconsistent column naming conventions and imputing missing values for water treatment methods.

Statistical Validation: Conducted correlation analysis to test the hypothesis that lead concentration is the leading indicator of infant mortality.

Visual Narrative (WATER POLLUTION 001_2.pbix): Developed a multi-stage dashboard in Power BI to communicate the debunking of initial assumptions and the identification of the primary variable driving survival trends.

Tech Stack
Programming Language: Python

Data Manipulation: Pandas, NumPy

Statistical Analysis: Seaborn, Matplotlib

Business Intelligence: Power BI (DAX, Visual Modeling, Trendline Analysis)

Research Findings
Toxicity Baseline: The majority of analyzed regions consistently exceed the World Health Organization safe limit of 10 µg/L for lead in water.

Data Paradox: Statistical modeling demonstrated a near-zero correlation between lead levels and infant mortality, indicating that pollution is not the sole driver of health outcomes.

The Hidden Variable: Trendline analysis revealed a strong inverse correlation between the Healthcare Access Index and Infant Mortality.

Conclusion: Medical infrastructure and healthcare access have historically outweighed the impacts of persistent water toxicity in the regions studied.
